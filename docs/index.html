Marker Assisted Selection

An interactive practical. No coding, no installing anything — just click, explore, and breed a better crop. Work through the steps from top to bottom.

? What are we doing here?

Plant breeders want to combine good traits — like disease resistance or high yield — into one crop variety.

Traditionally you cross plants and keep the ones that look best. That's slow: some traits only appear late in life, and some are expensive to measure (you'd have to flood a field with salt water just to see which plants survive!).

Genetic markers are a shortcut. A marker is a spot in the plant's DNA where individuals can differ. We simplify each marker to two values:

0 = the plant does not carry this DNA variant 1 = the plant does carry it

If a marker is reliably linked to a trait we care about, we can test a tiny leaf sample from a seedling instead of waiting months. That's marker assisted selection (MAS). Below, you'll use it to make a high-yielding crop salt resistant — without losing its yield.

1 Explore a diverse population

To find which marker controls salt resistance, we first need plants that differ from each other — genetically and in the trait. If every plant were identical, we'd have nothing to compare.

2 Find the marker with a GWAS

A Genome-Wide Association Study (GWAS) tests every marker separately: do plants with a 1 here tend to have a different trait value than plants with a 0?

Each test gives a p-value — small means the difference is unlikely to be pure chance. We plot -log10(p-value), so tiny p-values become tall bars. The taller the bar, the stronger the evidence. Tall bars sticking up like a skyline give this a Manhattan plot.

3 Meet the two parents

We found the salt-resistance marker. Now we want to move it into a crop farmers actually grow. We start from two very different plants:

Notice the trade-off: the resilient donor survives salt but yields little; the elite variety yields a lot but dies in salt. Our goal for the rest of this practical: break that trade-off — keep the elite's yield and gain the donor's salt resistance.

4 Marker assisted backcrossing

The trick: cross with the elite parent again and again (backcrossing), but each round keep only the offspring that still carry the salt-resistance marker. Over rounds, the plant becomes more and more like the elite variety — while never losing the one gene we care about.

5 Why yield is harder: polygenic traits

Salt resistance here is controlled by essentially one marker (a monogenic trait) — easy for MAS. Yield is polygenic: many genes each add a little.

Go back to the Manhattan plot in step 2 and switch the trait to Yield. Instead of one giant bar, you see many medium bars. There's no single marker to select on — which is exactly why breeders needed a different method: genomic selection (that's the next practical).
